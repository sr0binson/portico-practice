Troubleshooting Reference
Tier 1 support: confirm what you can see, document it clearly, escalate what you can't fix. Click each module to expand.
Your job as Tier 1: Confirm what you can see in the platform. If it looks wrong or you've hit your limit, document everything and escalate. You are not expected to fix backend systems or trigger syncs yourself.
How Portico's systems connect: Verity IQ (CRM/SIS) is the core. Campus Ivy (FA) pulls student records from it. CourseKey (Attendance/Payments) syncs with SIS. Trajecsys (Clinical) is separate but connected. If something breaks, it often breaks at a sync point between two systems -- that's Engineering territory.

The 5-Step Framework for Any Ticket

Use this on every ticket regardless of module

⌄
Step 1
Identify the system
Which Portico module is involved? CRM (Verity Dialog), SIS (Verity Student), FA (Campus Ivy Core), Docs (Verity Paperless Campus), Attendance/Skills (CourseKey), Interventions (Smart Nudges), or Clinical (Trajecsys)?
Step 2
Identify who is affected
Is this one user, one campus, one role, or all users? One person = likely a permissions or user error. Everyone = likely a sync failure, bug, or outage. Scope tells you urgency.
Step 3
Ask the clarifying questions
What exactly did they try to do? What happened instead? What did the error message say (exact text or code)? Has this happened before? Did anything change recently?
Step 4
Try the basic checks first
Log out and back in. Clear cache/cookies. Try a different browser. Confirm their role and permissions look correct. Check if the student or record exists in the upstream system (usually Verity Student). These are things you can see and verify yourself.
Step 5
Document and escalate if needed
If basic checks don't resolve it: write down what you confirmed, what the error was, which system, how many users affected, and whether it's SLA-sensitive. Escalate to Engineering with full context. A half-described ticket wastes everyone's time.
Verity Dialog

CRM -- Verity Dialog

Admissions, leads, enrollment pipeline, outreach

⌄
What this module does
Tracks prospective students from first inquiry through enrollment. Used mostly by admissions staff. Shows lead status, contact history, enrollment stage, and team performance metrics.
Common issues you'll see
  • Lead status not updating after a call or email
  • Student showing up twice (duplicate record)
  • Admissions rep can't see their assigned leads
  • Analytics data looks wrong or not refreshing
  • New inquiry form not sending to the CRM
What you can check
Lead not updating: Confirm the rep saved the activity log. Check if their role has edit permissions in User Management. Try refreshing -- some updates are near-real-time, not instant.

Duplicate record: Confirm which is the correct record (usually most recent activity). Flag for admin to merge -- don't delete anything yourself.

Can't see leads: Check their user role and assignment settings. This is almost always a permissions issue you can identify and escalate.

Analytics wrong: Confirm the date range filter. If data looks missing entirely, note it and escalate -- don't try to diagnose the data source yourself.
Error codes for this module
401Session expired or wrong permissions. Have them log out and back in. If it persists, check their role in User Management and escalate if the role looks correct but the error continues.
404Record not found. The lead may have been deleted or they're searching with the wrong ID. Confirm the ID and escalate if the record should exist.
422Form validation error. A required field is missing or formatted wrong. Walk them through the required fields -- this one you can usually resolve without escalating.
Verity Student

SIS -- Verity Student

Student records, enrollment, grades, transcripts

⌄
What this module does
The central student database. Registrars use it. Stores enrollment status, grades, clock hours, academic records, and program info. Other modules (FA, CourseKey) pull from here -- if Verity Student has bad data, everything downstream will too.
Common issues you'll see
  • Student record missing or showing wrong program
  • Grade or hours not reflecting correctly
  • Transcript won't generate
  • Enrollment status wrong (active vs. withdrawn)
  • Student doesn't appear in CourseKey or Campus Ivy
What you can check
Student missing in another module: Always check Verity Student first. Confirm they're active and correctly enrolled. This is the source of truth -- if it's wrong here, that's what you document and escalate.

Grade/hours wrong: Confirm the data was entered (not saved as draft). Note whether it's a manual entry issue or a sync issue. Sync issues get escalated.

Transcript won't generate: Look at the record for anything obviously incomplete -- missing dates, missing status. Also check Campus Ivy for a financial hold. Document what you find and escalate if you can't identify a clear data gap.
Error codes for this module
500Server error. Document it and check if others are affected. If multiple users at the same school are seeing it, escalate to Engineering as a potential outage.
422Data validation failed. A required field is missing in the student record -- usually program, start date, or enrollment status. You can identify the gap; the school admin makes the fix.
Sync errorCourseKey data didn't push to SIS. Document when it was last working and how many students are affected, then escalate to Engineering -- restarting sync jobs is not a Tier 1 action.
Campus Ivy

Financial Aid -- Campus Ivy Core

FAFSA, Title IV, disbursements, SAP, COD

⌄
What this module does
Manages federal financial aid for students. FA Directors use it. Handles FAFSA processing, aid packaging, disbursements, SAP checks, and reporting to federal systems.
Why FA tickets are high priority
Financial aid issues directly affect whether students can stay enrolled. A blocked disbursement or SAP hold can mean a student can't pay tuition. Treat these as urgent and escalate faster than other ticket types.
Common issues you'll see
  • Student aid package not showing or calculating wrong
  • Disbursement not processing
  • SAP hold incorrectly applied
  • ISIR data not importing from FAFSA
  • Workflow item stuck (red column in Campus Ivy dashboard)
What you can check
Aid not showing: Check if the student is active in Verity Student. If enrollment looks fine, document it and escalate -- you're not diagnosing federal aid processing yourself.

Disbursement stuck: Note whether it's showing red in the workflow dashboard. Document the student IDs and escalate with urgency flag. Do not try to manually clear or re-submit disbursements.

SAP hold: Note it exists and flag for the FA Director to review -- never remove a hold yourself, even if it looks wrong.

ISIR not importing: This is a federal data connection issue. Document and escalate immediately -- don't touch federal data.
Key terms to know
ISIRInstitutional Student Information Record -- processed FAFSA data. Must be imported before aid can be packaged.
SAPSatisfactory Academic Progress. Students must meet GPA/completion thresholds to keep aid. A hold blocks disbursement.
CODCommon Origination and Disbursement -- the federal system schools report to. Errors here are escalated immediately.
R2T4Return to Title IV. When a student withdraws, the school may owe money back to the federal government.
Title IVFederal student aid programs (Pell, subsidized/unsubsidized loans). Trade schools are heavily dependent on this.
CourseKey

Attendance & Skills -- CourseKey

Clock hours, attendance, skills checklists, payments

⌄
What this module does
Tracks student attendance by clock hours (trade schools work in hours, not credits). Also tracks skills completion via checklists, handles payment processing, and feeds attendance data into Verity Student.
Common issues you'll see
  • Attendance hours not showing up in Verity Student
  • Student hours showing wrong total
  • Skills checklist won't save or submit
  • Student not appearing in CourseKey at all
  • Student can't clock in (mobile app issue)
What you can check
Student missing in CourseKey: Check Verity Student first. If they're active and enrolled there, the issue is a sync that hasn't run. Document and escalate -- you're not triggering the sync yourself.

Hours not showing in SIS: Note when the problem started and how many students are affected. This is a sync issue between CourseKey and Verity Student. Escalate to Engineering with that context.

Student can't clock in: Confirm they're enrolled in the correct program in CourseKey. Check if it's a device or app issue. If enrollment looks fine and the problem persists, escalate.
Error codes for this module
Webhook timeoutThe sync from CourseKey to Verity Student failed. Hours may be stuck. Document the timing and affected students and escalate to Engineering -- webhook issues are not Tier 1 to fix.
422Skills checklist submission failed -- usually a required field is blank. Walk the user through required fields before escalating.
Trajecsys

Clinical Management -- Trajecsys

Externships, preceptors, clinical hours, nursing programs

⌄
What this module does
Manages clinical rotations and externships for nursing and allied health programs. Tracks hours at clinical sites, preceptor assignments, and compliance documentation. Acquired by Portico late 2025 -- it's the newest system in the platform.
Common issues you'll see
  • Clinical hours not showing in student record
  • Preceptor not assigned or can't access a student's file
  • Compliance documents missing or not accepted
  • Clinical coordinator can't see a student's site assignment
What you can check
Clinical hours not showing: Confirm preceptors are logging hours (they enter them, not students). If hours exist in Trajecsys but aren't reaching Verity Student, that's a cross-system sync issue -- escalate to Engineering.

Preceptor access issue: Confirm their login was created and they have the correct site access. Preceptors are external users set up differently from school staff.

When in doubt: Trajecsys is the newest acquisition. If you're unsure, document what you can confirm and escalate. That's the right call.
Escalation

When and How to Escalate

What goes to Engineering vs. what you handle

⌄
Escalate immediately if:
  • A 500 error is happening for multiple users at the same school
  • A disbursement or financial aid action is blocked and time-sensitive
  • Attendance hours are not reaching SIS and basic checks look fine
  • Anything involving federal financial data (COD, NSLDS, ISIR)
  • An SLA breach is at 90% or higher on a high-priority ticket
Handle yourself first if:
  • It's a login issue (try logout/login, check role -- most 401s resolve this way)
  • It's a "where is my record" question (search with the correct ID)
  • It's a user who doesn't know how to do something (walk them through it)
  • It's a missing field the school admin can fill in themselves
What to include in an escalation
Ticket ID, school name, affected user(s), module, exact error message or code, steps you already tried, number of users affected, SLA status, and whether it's affecting live student enrollment or financial aid. The more context you give Engineering, the faster they can move.

Interview Drill
Read the question. Think of your answer. Then reveal the suggested response and what to avoid.
J
Joshua
Senior Support Analyst -- will ask about how you think through problems, not Portico knowledge you couldn't have yet
Walk me through how you approach a problem you've never seen before.
Why he asks: He wants to know your process. Not your Portico knowledge -- your instincts.
I start by figuring out what I actually know vs. what I'm assuming. At Boise, when I hit a device issue I hadn't seen, I'd confirm the symptoms first -- what's happening, what's not happening, who else is affected. Then I'd work through the basics before I escalated: is it the device, the user, the network, the software? I document as I go so if it does go up to someone else, they're not starting from zero. I don't guess and I don't freeze -- I just keep narrowing it down.
Avoid: Saying you'd Google it or just escalate right away. Show that you have a process before you hand it off.
Tell me about a time you had to figure something out with no documentation or training.
Why he asks: Portico is a newer merged platform. You will hit undocumented situations. He wants to know you won't freeze.
When I built the support dashboard for my portfolio, I was working with the Airtable API and Make.com to build escalation logic from scratch -- there wasn't a guide for exactly what I was trying to do. I had to read the API docs, test small pieces, and work backward from the errors I got. That approach -- isolate the variable, test one thing at a time, read the error carefully -- is how I'd work through an unfamiliar support issue too.
Avoid: A vague story. Name the actual thing you figured out and how you got there.
What's the difference between a 401 error and a 500 error?
Why he asks: Basic technical competency check. This is general IT knowledge, not Portico-specific.
A 401 is an authorization error -- the user doesn't have permission or their session expired. I'd start with logout and back in, then check their role and permissions. A 500 is a server-side error that's not caused by the user -- something broke on the backend. One person getting a 500 might be a fluke, but if multiple people are seeing it at the same time, that's a potential outage and I'd escalate to Engineering right away with as much detail as I have.
Avoid: Saying both mean "something is broken." The distinction matters and he's testing whether you know it.
How do you decide when to escalate vs. keep troubleshooting yourself?
Why he asks: He wants to know you won't over-escalate (creates noise) or under-escalate (drops the ball).
I escalate when I've hit the edge of what I can actually see or fix. At Boise, my job was to confirm what I could verify -- is the device the problem, is it the user, is it the network -- and then hand it off clearly when it went beyond that. I wouldn't escalate something I could resolve with a permissions check or a browser refresh. But if I've done the basics and the problem is clearly in a system I don't have access to or authority over, I escalate fast with full notes so whoever picks it up doesn't have to start over.
Avoid: "I'd always try to fix it myself first." That's not the right frame -- knowing your limits is part of the job.
How do you handle a queue where two tickets are about to breach SLA and three others are waiting?
Why he asks: Prioritization under pressure is a real daily skill in this role.
The SLA-breaching ones go first, starting with whichever has the higher impact -- something affecting a student's enrollment or financial aid beats a reporting question. For those tickets, my first goal is acknowledgment and triage, not full resolution -- even a quick quality response to let the client know I'm on it buys time and keeps them calm. Then I'd work through the remaining three by urgency and complexity, and combine any that look like the same underlying issue.
Avoid: "First in, first out." That's not how SLA-aware support works.
How do you document a ticket you're handing off to Engineering?
Why he asks: Bad handoffs waste Engineering's time. He wants to know you write clean escalations.
I'd include: the ticket ID, the school and user affected, the module, exactly what error they saw (word for word if possible), what I already tried, how many people are affected, and whether there's any time sensitivity -- like if it's tied to financial aid or an SLA that's close to breaching. I built a macro for this at Boise to make sure I didn't skip anything. The goal is that Engineering can pick it up without asking me a single follow-up question.
Avoid: Vague handoffs like "user is having an issue with the system." That's not useful to anyone.
T
Tina
Customer Success Manager -- will test your communication, empathy, and client relationship skills
How do you communicate bad news to a client -- like when a fix is going to take longer than expected?
Why she asks: She wants to know you won't go silent or overpromise.
I'd reach out proactively -- before they have to follow up. I'd tell them what I know, what's still being worked on, and give a realistic timeframe if I have one. If I don't have a timeline yet, I'd say that clearly rather than guessing. I'd also tell them what I'm doing in the meantime and when to expect the next update. The goal is that they never wonder if their issue was forgotten.
Avoid: "I'd escalate and let them know when it's fixed." That's passive. Tina wants to hear that you stay in contact yourself.
Tell me about a time you turned a frustrated customer into a satisfied one.
Why she asks: She wants empathy AND a real example.
At Trader Joe's I was promoted to Mate within 6 weeks, which meant I was handling customer complaints on the floor every shift. There was a customer who came in upset about a product issue -- I didn't argue, I just listened first and made sure she felt heard before I did anything. Then I solved the actual problem quickly. She came back later that day to tell my captain I was the reason she'd keep shopping with us. Listening before fixing has carried into everything I've done since.
Avoid: A vague story. Be specific and show it was your actions that changed the outcome.
What do you know about Portico and why do you want to work here specifically?
Why she asks: She's testing whether you did your homework and whether you're actually interested.
Portico launched in May 2025 by merging four companies -- Verity IQ, Campus Ivy, CourseKey, and Trajecsys -- into one platform for career-focused schools. You serve over 300 institutions and 400,000 students in trade, nursing, allied health, and beauty programs. What stood out to me is that this isn't generic EdTech -- it's built for students trying to get into a career, not a four-year degree. That matters. I also built a platform simulator to study your modules before this interview. I want to work here because the work is real and the impact on students is direct.
Avoid: "I saw it on LinkedIn and it seemed like a good fit." Show you researched them.
How do you handle a situation where a client is demanding something you can't actually give them?
Why she asks: This happens constantly in B2B SaaS support.
I'd acknowledge what they're asking for and why it makes sense from their side. Then I'd be honest about what I can and can't do -- I wouldn't promise something just to end the conversation. If there's a workaround that gets them most of what they need, I'd offer that. If it's a feature request, I'd let them know I'll pass it to the Product team so it's on record. The goal is for them to feel like their ask was heard and taken seriously, even if the answer is no right now.
Avoid: "I'd just escalate it." That's not managing the client relationship.
What do you think makes someone successful in a role like this?
Why she asks: She's looking for self-awareness and cultural fit.
Curiosity and follow-through. In a platform still being unified from four different systems, there's always something you don't know yet -- so you have to be willing to dig in and learn fast. But curiosity without follow-through just creates noise. The schools you support are dealing with real student outcomes, so they need someone who says "I'll get back to you by end of day" and actually does. I also think transparency matters -- being honest about what you don't know yet builds more trust than pretending.
Avoid: "I'm a hard worker and fast learner." That's generic. Tie it to something specific about Portico.
Wildcard Prep
Unexpected things that might come up. Be ready for these.
These are curveballs -- questions or moments that can catch you off guard if you haven't thought about them. Read each one and think through your answer before the interview.
Role Play
"Let's do a quick role play. I'm a school administrator and I can't log in to the system. Go."
How to handle it: Don't panic. Treat it like a real ticket. Start with: "Thanks for reaching out -- let's get this sorted. Can you tell me what error you're seeing when you try to log in?" Then work through it: 401 = permissions or session issue. No error = possibly wrong URL or browser issue. Account locked = check with their admin. Stay calm, be methodical, don't rush to a solution before you have the info.
Silence
"What questions do you have for us?" (and you go blank)
Backup questions to always have ready: "What does a successful first 90 days look like in this role?" / "What's the most common type of ticket your team handles right now?" / "How does the support team collaborate with Engineering when something needs to be escalated?" / "How is Portico approaching the integration of the four platforms over the next year?" Any of these show you're thinking about the job, not just the offer.
Weakness Question
"What's a weakness you're actively working on?"
How to handle it: Don't say "I work too hard" or "I'm a perfectionist." Be real. A strong answer: "I'm still building depth on enterprise SaaS platforms -- I've been studying Portico's platform specifically and built a simulator to practice, but I know there's a learning curve and I'm committed to getting up to speed fast." That turns a gap into evidence of your work ethic.
Experience Gap
"You don't have direct SaaS support experience. Why should we hire you?"
How to handle it: Don't apologize for your resume. "My IT internship gave me real troubleshooting experience across 12 school sites and Zendesk. I built a platform simulator and studied your knowledge base to prepare for this role. I know I'm not coming in with years of SaaS experience, but I'm coming in with the instincts, the prep, and the drive -- and I don't think you'll find someone who's done more to get ready for this specific job."
Compensation
"Are you okay with the $58k salary range?"
How to handle it: The listed salary is $58k. If you're comfortable with that, a simple "Yes, that works for me" is fine. If you want to negotiate later, now is not the time -- lock in the offer first. You can say: "I'm focused on finding the right role and company -- I'm confident we can make it work if there's a mutual fit."
The Simulator
If they ask you to show your portfolio or walk them through your work
How to handle it: Lead with the Portico platform simulator. Explain: "I built this to learn your modules before the interview -- it covers all seven modules including the student and admin views, ticket queue with SLA tracking, error codes, and canned responses." Then mention the support dashboard as a separate portfolio piece showing SLA tracking and escalation logic. That's a strong one-two showing both platform prep and independent building.
Culture Fit
"How would you describe your work style?"
How to handle it: Tie it to Portico's culture. "I'm a self-starter -- I don't wait for someone to tell me what to learn. I built prep tools for this interview on my own. I'm also a team player: I know support doesn't work in isolation and I'd want to be useful to Engineering and Customer Success, not just the people filing tickets. And I'm customer-first -- at Trader Joe's that was the whole job, and I carried it into my IT work too."
